## Lesson 01 in reverse

In lesson 01 you saw when one prompt isn't enough. Now, before learning to build chains, <Character name="orbit" /> stops you cold: **most tasks aren't multi-step**. If you split everything, you'll pile on cost, latency, and bugs without gaining anything.

> Orbit's rule: *one good single prompt beats five mediocre chained prompts*. Multi-step pays when the problem **really** branches. Not when you imagine it could.

### The four "don't split" signals

1. **Output = direct transformation of the input.** Format strings, parsing a known JSON, literal translation. One model call or one pure function. Not three.
2. **Validatable with regex/parser.** If success is checkable in code, you don't need a judge or a chain. A well-written prompt plus a deterministic test is everything.
3. **Short, focusable prompt.** If all the rules fit in 10-15 lines without contradicting each other, one call handles them.
4. **No external data.** No retrieval, no tools, no conditional on the world. If everything needed is in the input, there's nothing to orchestrate.

### Real costs of over-splitting

Every step you add brings:

- **Latency.** 200-2000ms per LLM call. Three steps = possible 5-second response.
- **Cost.** Each step charges tokens in + out. Three steps repeat the same context three times.
- **Failure points.** Each step can fail, hallucinate, or return an unexpected format.
- **Maintenance.** Three prompts to maintain, three rubrics to write, three versions to track.

> If the task fits in one prompt and you can validate it with regex, **keep it in one prompt**. Complexity is earned, not gifted.

### The decision trick

Before you split, write the task in one line. If the line has **one real decision**, it stays in one prompt. If it has several genuinely independent decisions (classify AND extract AND summarize), then split.

Look at the two designs on the right. One is honest: one decision, one prompt. The other invents steps to look like engineering. Pick the one you'd ship.
