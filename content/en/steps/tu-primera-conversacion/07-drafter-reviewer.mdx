## Two roles, one conversation

The model is better at **critiquing** a draft than at **writing the perfect draft**. That asymmetry gets exploited with a classic pattern: the drafter-reviewer loop.

### The pattern

```
Turn N:   "Write me X."                          → DRAFTER produces
Turn N+1: "Now you're <reviewer>. List 3 weak
           things. Don't rewrite."               → REVIEWER critiques
Turn N+2: "Attack points 1 and 3 with these
           corrections..."                       → DRAFTER fixes
```

Same model. Same conversation. The role swap between turns is the only thing that changes. And that alone raises output quality significantly.

### Why it works

When the model writes, it's optimizing "produce something plausible". When you ask it to switch role and critique, it's optimizing "find flaws". Two different cognitive modes. no human can run both at the same time, the model can't either.

> Useful variant: the reviewer can be a **specific persona**. *"You're Atlas reading this at 6 AM."*. *"You're a skeptical customer evaluating this proposal."*. *"You're a security linter."* The more concrete the role, the more concrete the findings.

### What DOESN'T work

- **"Make it better."** Zero criteria. Cosmetic changes.
- **"Rewrite it and fix what's wrong."** The model picks and rarely lands right.
- **"Make it more professional."** Vague adjective = a coat of varnish.

### What does

- **"You're <concrete role>. List 3 weak things. Don't rewrite yet."**

The "don't rewrite yet" matters: if you let it rewrite in the same turn, it re-enters drafter mode and the critique gets diluted into a new version.

> Tip: when you ask for critiques, ask for a NUMBER ("3 things"). If you say "list the weaknesses", the model brings 1 or 12, no commitment. With a fixed number, it forces a pick of the most important ones.
