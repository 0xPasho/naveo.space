## The pink elephant of LLMs

If I tell you *"don't think about a pink elephant"*, what are you thinking about? Exactly. LLMs have a stronger version of the same effect.

Every word you put in the prompt enters the context and becomes a **more available** token for generation. If you repeat "names" five times in negative instructions, the model actually **has "names" more loaded** than if you had never mentioned it.

### The accidental reinforcement rule

The more you insist on *"DON'T say X"*:

- X appears more times in your prompt.
- X enters the model's context as an available token.
- The model learns that X is **relevant** (otherwise, why insist so hard).
- It sometimes ends up saying X **more** than if you had said nothing.

It doesn't always happen. But it's a real risk, especially when the restriction shows up 3+ times.

### How to say NO without reinforcing

**Pattern 1: a single clear mention.**

```
DO NOT include proper names in the summary.
```

Once, up front, and never again. More mentions raise the risk without adding clarity.

**Pattern 2: positive reframing.**

```
Use generic references ('the crewmate', 'the officer') instead of proper names.
```

Instead of forbidding, state what to do. The model gets a **concrete alternative** that replaces the unwanted behavior.

**Pattern 3: structural constraint.**

```
The output must not contain any capitalized word except at the start of
a sentence.
```

You convert the semantic restriction into a syntactic one. Easier for the model to respect and easier for you to verify.

### When negatives DO work

Not everything is a trap. Negatives work well when:

- They are **a single** mention (not repeated).
- The forbidden behavior is **very specific** ("don't use double quotes").
- The negative **doesn't name** the problematic concept by its exact name ("don't add extra context" instead of "don't mention X").

> Quick heuristic: if your prompt has the same negative 3+ times, rethink. There's probably a positive form that says the same thing in one line.
