## Why models are bad at counting

LLMs **don't count well**. They don't have an internal calculator. When you tell them "max 50 words", they're approximating, not measuring. That's why length constraints need more care than other rules.

### Three constraints that work better than "max N words"

**1. Characters instead of words.**

```
"Max 120 characters."
```

Better than words because models are more consistent with the unit. Still not exact, but the error is ±20% rather than ±50%.

**2. Forced structure instead of length.**

```
"Exactly one sentence."
"A list of 3 items, no more, no fewer."
"Two paragraphs: first the what, second the why."
```

Structure is easier to respect than a number. If your prompt says "exactly 3 bullets", the model almost always returns 3.

**3. Specific prohibitions.**

```
"DO NOT use exclamation marks."
"DO NOT include explanations after the answer."
"DO NOT use bullet lists."
```

Each specific prohibition cuts off a common "elaboration" branch the model would take.

### The scaffold trick

If you want exactly one sentence, **end the prompt with a visual cue**:

```
Notification: 
```

(with the space and the colon, no value). The model treats that as the "scaffold" to write on top of and tends to respect the format.

### Why vague lengths fail

*"Be concise"* is completely subjective. *"One sentence"* is precise. The difference is what the model can check against its output as it generates. Rules that can be verified in real time work; aesthetic rules don't.

> Practical validation: use regex to check structure (sentence count, presence of bullets, line breaks). Use length-between N and M characters as a hard limit. Leave the judge for content quality.
