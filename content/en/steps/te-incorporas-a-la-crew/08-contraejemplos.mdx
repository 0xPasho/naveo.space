## Showing what NOT to do

Normal few-shot shows you *"do this"*. Counter-examples show you *"don't do this"*. Sometimes the second teaches more, especially when the model has a natural tendency to fail in a specific direction.

### When counter-examples help

Counter-examples shine when:

1. **The model has a wrong default** you want to avoid.
2. **The distinction is subtle**: the correct and incorrect answers look very similar.
3. **You've already tried positive-only** and it fails on the predictable case.

Example: extracting just the alias from a bio. Without counter-examples, the model sometimes returns the full name. With a "WRONG: full name / CORRECT: alias" pair, the distinction is explicit.

### Three ways to write them

**1. Direct label, side by side.**

```
Bio: "María Pérez (alias 'Em')"
✓ CORRECT: Em
✗ WRONG: María Pérez
```

Simple and clear. The model sees the distinction next to the input.

**2. In the description.**

```
Extract the alias (NOT the full name).
If the bio says "María Pérez (alias 'Em')", return "Em", not "María Pérez".
```

Useful when you just want to prevent one specific error without adding extra examples.

**3. Separate "anti-patterns" block.**

```xml
<examples>
  Bio: "..." → Alias: "Em"
</examples>

<avoid>
  Don't return the full name.
  Don't return values wrapped in quotes.
  Don't explain your reasoning.
</avoid>
```

For tasks with several possible failure modes.

### The important catch: careful with reinforcing the bad pattern

There's a weird LLM phenomenon: if you insist heavily on *"DON'T say X"*, sometimes the model says it more. The phrase "don't mention the weather" can **increase** the probability of mentioning the weather, because "weather" entered the context and became an available token.

Practical rule: **one negative mention is fine, repeating it 3 times raises the risk**. If your prompt has "don't do X" in 4 places, rethink: it's probably more effective to positively state what SHOULD happen.

> This is one of the better-known LLM traps (we'll come back to it in lesson 17). For now: counter-examples yes, in moderation, and always with a visible label.
