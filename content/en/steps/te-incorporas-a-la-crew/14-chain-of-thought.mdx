## The model is smarter if you let it think

By default, LLMs **guess fast and may be wrong**. If you ask "cause of this symptom", they tend to fire off the first hypothesis that comes to mind.

**Chain of thought** (CoT) is the technique of asking the model to **show its reasoning step by step before** concluding. It's not magic: it's giving the model the space (literally, tokens) to process.

It works because every token the model generates is a step of compute. If you force it to generate analysis first, it's "warming up" the answer before committing to a conclusion.

### See it side by side

Same symptom, two prompts.

**Without CoT:**

```
Prompt: "Probable cause of: the auxiliary generator shuts down 10 seconds after starting."

Response: "Probably an issue with the voltage regulator."
```

The model fired off the first hypothesis that came out. Plausible, but why that one and not another? Impossible to verify.

**With CoT:**

```
Prompt: "Reason step by step before concluding.
Analysis:
1. ...
2. ...
3. ...

Probable cause: ..."

Response:
Analysis:
1. A shutdown at 10 seconds suggests the system starts but detects something in the first cycles.
2. Protection that trips at 10s is usually thermal or oil pressure.
3. If it were the voltage regulator, the symptom would be oscillation, not a clean shutdown.

Probable cause: thermal protection triggering due to oil sensor failure or low oil.
```

The second analysis **actively ruled out** the regulator hypothesis (the one the model gave without CoT) and landed on a more specific one. It's not magic: those were 3 extra generation steps the first run didn't have.

> When you let the model write "1, 2, 3" before the conclusion, you're explicitly giving it room to reason. The conclusion that comes after uses all that "evidence" as context.

### The task

<Character name="forge" /> maintains the ship's systems. When a strange symptom appears, she doesn't want a hunch. she wants to see **how** the model got to the conclusion, so she can verify it.

Your prompt must produce two marked sections:

```
Analysis:
1. ...
2. ...
3. ...

Probable cause: ...
```

The validator checks:

1. The `Analysis:` section exists.
2. The `Probable cause:` section exists.
3. The analysis is **actually** step by step (not a single statement).
4. The cause is plausible for the symptom.

### The key detail

There are two ways to ask for reasoning:

**Weak:** _"Think step by step before answering."_ (sometimes works, sometimes gets ignored)

**Strong:** showing the exact format in `<output_format>`:

```
Analysis:
1. ...
2. ...
3. ...

Probable cause: ...
```

The second one always works because the model treats the format as a contract. The structure IS the chain of thought.
