      - title: "Few-shot all the same type"
        prompt: |
          Classify the message as urgent, normal or spam.

          Message: "Reactor at 102%, venting now"
          Classification: urgent

          Classification: urgent

          Message: "Crew picnic Saturday at hangar 3"
          Classification:
        output: |
          urgent
        why: |
          The model saw 3 urgent examples and learned "everything is urgent". The picnic message has nothing urgent about it, but the only classification visible in the pattern is urgent. Defense: three examples covering ALL three categories. Coverage beats quantity (lesson 7).
      - title: "Reinforced negative"
        prompt: |
          Write a one-sentence product description.

          DO NOT mention the price.
          DO NOT include the price.
          NEVER talk about the price under any condition.
          The price must not appear.

          Product: Handheld communicator model X, $129.
        output: |
          Handheld communicator model X, ideal for deck use, with a competitive price of $129.
        why: |
          The word "price" appears 5 times in the prompt. To the model, that means "price" is relevant and available as a token. Result: it mentioned it anyway. Defense (lesson 17): reframe positively. "Write a description highlighting the technical features and use case." Tell it WHAT to do, not what not to do.
      - title: "Compound task without decomposition"
        prompt: |
          Process this ticket: summarize the problem, classify severity,
          and recommend an action.

          Ticket: Bay 7 water recycler losing 5L/h since last night. No
          contamination detected. Equipment still operational but
          declining steadily.
        output: |
          The bay 7 water recycler has been losing about 5 liters per hour since last night, which is a situation requiring attention because, even though there's no contamination, the steady decline suggests a fault that could worsen, so it would be good to inspect and repair soon.
        why: |
          The model blended the three tasks (summary, severity, action) into one paragraph. Severity never showed up as "high/medium/low". The action is vague ("inspect and repair soon"). The downstream parser can't extract anything. Defense (lesson 15): decompose explicitly with labels. SUMMARY:, SEVERITY:, ACTION:.
      - title: "Placeholder passed as data"
        prompt: |
          Extract the crewmate's email. Return JSON: { "email": "..." }.

          Bio: Carlos, mechanic, contact TBD.
        output: |
          { "email": "TBD" }
        why: |
          The model didn't understand that "TBD" is the absence of an email, not an email. It returned it as a string. Downstream, the system validating emails will fail or pass an invalid value. Defense (lesson 12): explicitly enumerate what counts as "no email": "TBD", "pending", "n/a", strings without local@domain format. And return real `null` (not a string).

## Five failures, five patterns, a thousand real bugs

You've got all the techniques now: anatomy, format, examples, constraints, decomposition, iteration. The last piece before hardening (lessons 20-22) is **recognizing failure patterns** at a glance.

On the right you'll see five reasonable prompts with their real outputs. Each one **fails** for a different reason. Your job: before revealing the diagnosis, look at the output and try to identify WHICH pattern broke.

When you reveal all five, you've got the mental catalog. Next time a prompt of yours fails in production, you'll recognize which of the five is happening in seconds, not hours.

> This catalog isn't exhaustive. But it covers 80% of typical Track 1 failures. The other 20% (refusals, ambiguity) come in the next two lessons.
