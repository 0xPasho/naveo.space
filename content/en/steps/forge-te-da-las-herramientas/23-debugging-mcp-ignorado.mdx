  explanation: |
    It's a textbook case where **the three causes reinforce each other**:

    **1. Opaque name.** "do_thing" says nothing. The model picks tools by
    association between user intent and name + description. If the name
    adds nothing, it depends 100% on the description.

    **2. Empty description.** "Does a thing" is equivalent to having no
    description. The model has no when-YES, no when-NO, no what-it-returns.
    By default, it doesn't invoke.

    **3. Toxic parameters.** 5 required parameters, all `object` with no
    `properties` defined. The model doesn't know what to put in
    `metadata`, `config`, `options`, `context`. It's indistinguishable
    from noise. When the model doesn't know how to build the arguments,
    **it prefers not to invoke** rather than invoke wrong.

    In production, any of the three by itself is enough for a tool to be
    ignored. The three together are a classic case of "tool built without
    thinking about the LLM as the consumer".

    How to fix:

    - **Concrete name.** If the tool files an incident, it's called
      `file_incident_report`, not `do_thing`.
    - **Description that says what, when YES, when NO** (step 5).
    - **Minimal, typed parameters.** Only the essentials in `required`.
      If you need an object, define its `properties` with descriptions.

## When the agent acts like your tool doesn't exist

The symptom is always the same: the tool is registered, the handler works, but the agent **never invokes it**. Types of problems:

- Under-invocation (low recall): should use it, doesn't.
- Over-invocation (low precision): uses it when it shouldn't.
- Invoked with bad arguments: breaks and gets discarded.

Your job in this step: read a schema that's being ignored and pick the most likely root cause.

### The three dimensions where a tool can fail

1. **The name.** Does it communicate intent? `lookup_crewmate` vs `find_data`.
2. **The description.** Does it say when YES, when NO, what it guarantees?
3. **The parameters.** Are they minimal? Typed? Do they have `description`s?

When a tool fails in one of these, it's already a candidate to be ignored. When it fails in all three, it's **practically invisible** to the agent.

Look at the spec on the right and pick the most likely cause.

> In production, this is the debugging of 80% of MCPs that "don't work": it's not broken code, it's **poorly documented for the LLM**.
