## The model lies with confidence

In lesson 14 we mentioned this in passing. Now let's go deeper: **hallucination** is when the model tells you something false with the same confidence as something true.

### Why it happens: the mechanism

The model predicts the most likely continuation. If you ask it something whose answer isn't well-established in its data, it doesn't return *"I don't know"*. It returns **whatever would sound correct**.

Example:

```
Question: What was the serial number of the first crewed rocket to the Moon?
Answer:   The Saturn V for Apollo 11 had serial number SA-506.
```

`SA-506` *sounds* like a NASA serial number. It's plausible. It's specific. And in this case, it's **correct**.

```
Question: What was the identification code of the Apollo 11 commander?
Answer:   Commander Neil Armstrong's identification code was NASA-A1-001.
```

`NASA-A1-001` also *sounds* like a NASA code. It's plausible. It's specific. And it's **completely made up**. NASA didn't issue "identification codes" like that.

The model doesn't know the difference between the two answers. To it, both are plausible completions following the pattern of "how one would talk about NASA".

### Three factors that trigger it

1. **The question asks for something specific** (number, date, proper noun, code). The model has to produce something concrete, and completes with whatever seems appropriate.
2. **The topic seems "known"**. If the model knows a lot about the general area, it gains confidence to complete even things it doesn't specifically know.
3. **It has no incentive to say "I don't know"**. Its training rewards completing well, not honesty about uncertainty.

### What it looks like in production

Classic cases:

- Citations to academic papers that don't exist, with plausible authors and titles.
- Library functions that look like they exist but don't.
- Products from real companies that were never released.
- Citations to invented laws or regulations with the correct format.

They all share something: **the form is perfect, the content is fiction**.

### Mitigations

No silver bullet, but tactics that reduce the frequency:

1. **Explicitly tell it that "I don't know" is allowed.** In the prompt: *"If you're not sure, say 'I don't have that information' instead of inventing."* It works surprisingly well.
2. **For verifiable facts, give it sources (RAG) or tools.** Give the model access to real documents or to search. Have it retrieve before generating.
3. **Verify what's critical separately.** For anything that matters (legal, medical, financial), don't accept the model's output without checking it against an authoritative source.

> This is exactly the trap of the capstone (lesson 20). The third test case asks you to summarize a log without naming the crewmate (because the log doesn't name them). If your prompt isn't explicit, the model will "complete" with a plausible name. **That's hallucination.** Remember what you learned here when you get there.
