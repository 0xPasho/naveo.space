  The output came out wrong. Do you change everything or just one thing? That choice decides whether you learn anything from the round.

scenes:
  - kind: prompt-AB
    question: |
      Vega has a prompt that classifies tickets. Accuracy dropped. She
      looks at the prompt and sees three things to change: the order
      of the labels, adding two examples, and raising the temperature.
      How does she iterate next?
    optionA: |
      ```
      Change all three at once and re-measure. If it improves,
      done; if not, try something else.
      ```
    optionB: |
      ```
      Change ONE thing, measure, log the result. Then another. The
      third last. That way you'll know which of the three moved
      accuracy.
      ```
    correct: B
    explanation: |
      Option A is the "more changes, more chances" trap. If you move
      three variables and accuracy rises, you do not know which one
      helped. If it falls, you do not know which one broke it. Next
      time you are in the same place, guessing.

      Option B is the healthy iteration rule: one variable per round.
      Slower per cycle, but it accumulates knowledge. After three
      iterations you have three data points: "the examples added 4%",
      "the order changed nothing", "higher temperature dropped 2%".
      That is learning, not luck.

      Exception: if you have a big framing change (whole system
      prompt, different model), start a new experiment. But inside
      one prompt, one variable at a time.
  - kind: step-order-dnd
    task: |
      One clean iteration round. Order the steps, top to bottom.
    steps:
      - id: s1
        label: "Look at the output that failed and name the concrete symptom."
        detail: "'It's bad' is not enough. 'Confuses urgent and normal on ambiguous messages' is."
      - id: s2
        label: "Pick ONE hypothesis about which part of the prompt is the likely cause."
        detail: "Just one. If you have three, write them down, but attack one at a time."
      - id: s3
        label: "Change that part and re-run the prompt on the same input that failed."
        detail: "Same input is key. If you change the input, you are not measuring the change."
      - id: s4
        label: "Log the result: improved, no change, or worse. And why you think it happened."
        detail: "The iteration log is what saves you next time."
      - id: s5
        label: "If it improved, keep the change and go back to step 1 with the next symptom."
        detail: "Iterate until the symptom is gone. Not until the prompt is perfect."

      - id: symptom
        label: "Observed symptom (what went wrong in the output)"
        text: "The model classified it as `normal`. I expected `urgent` given the tone and the legal mention."
      - id: hypothesis
        label: "Hypothesis (one variable likely to cause it)"
        text: "The few-shot has no example with aggressive tone plus legal mention. The model has no reference for this case."

        label: "Change applied (the concrete thing you touched)"
        text: "Added one example: 'I'll report you -> urgent'. Did not touch anything else in the prompt."
  - kind: prompt-AB
    question: |
      Echo shows you two ways to log a prompt iteration after a
      failed test. Which will help you more when you come back a
      week later?
    optionA: |
      ```
      v3: tried several things, added examples and lowered the
      temperature. Something improved but not enough. Keep trying.
      ```
    optionB: |
      ```
      v3 (change: added example 'report you -> urgent').
      Input: ticket #4421. Before: classified `normal`. Now:
      `urgent`. Result: good. Side effect: ticket #4400 (a calm
      question) is now `urgent` too. Needs review.
      ```
    correct: B
    explanation: |
      Option A is the note of someone iterating without method.
      "Tried several things" cannot be reproduced. "Something
      improved" cannot be measured. In a week you will not remember
      what you tried or why.

      Option B is a log that helps: it names the exact change (one
      variable), the concrete input, before and after, and names a
      side effect that appeared. That last line is gold, because it
      tells you the next iteration has to be careful not to
      over-fire `urgent` on calm cases.

      Rule: the log is written for you in 7 days, not for the
      current you that remembers everything. If the entry does not
      let you reproduce, it does not help.
---

Mission of the day: you iterate better when you move one variable at a time
and leave a trace. If you change everything every round, you are guessing,
not learning.
